Wheeless Cleared of any Wrongdoing in 2009 Scholastic;
Accord Reached with Fuerstman and Hales

by Wilder Wadford, NCCA Attorney
July 24, 2009

The Board has conducted an internal audit of the finances for the 2009 Scholastic Tournament which was an NCCA sponsored event organized by Randy Wheeless, former President and First Vice President of the NCCA and Communications Manager for Duke Energy. The Board’s unanimous conclusion, with the concurrence of myself, Wilder Wadford, attorney for the NCCA, is that there were no financial improprieties and that Mr. Wheeless is to be congratulated for a job well done which resulted in the most profitable tournament in the entire history of the NCCA.

The internal audit was conducted by the following committee: myself, Wilder Wadford, the NCCA’a attorney with thirty five years of experience in business matters, past two time President of the NCCA, former legal liaison to the USCF, former delegate to the USCF, many times past President of the Asheville Chess Club, winner of the USCF Meritorious Service Award, organizer of the Land of the Sky Tournament and NCCA Hall of Fame member; Gary Newsom, First Vice President of the NCCA, organizer of the Queen City Chess Club and a successful small businessman, and Gerben Hoekstra, three term Secretary Treasurer of the NCCA, Past President of the CMSCA and organizer of scholastic tournaments, former USCF delegate, assistant to many major tournaments including the World Open and for many years my assistant at the Land of the Sky, and former District Manager for General Electric Power Generation. Randy Wheeless fully cooperated with the committee.

It is the Board’s and my considered and deliberate opinion that Mr. Wheeless has committed no malfeasance or misfeasance and that the finances of the tournament were appropriately handled leading to the greatest financial tournament success in NCCA history.

A complete disclosure of the conduct and findings of that audit are included in this article.

                                                 Background Statement

Commencing March 30, 2009, Leland Fuerstman made allegations of possible misconduct by Mr. Wheeless in a number of long articles in public chess forums on the internet, including his own chess club website and the NCCA’s website.  Mr. Fuerstman publicly included Tom Hales as an additional source of information concerning Mr. Fuerstman’s allegations. On May 5, 2009, Mr. Wheeless filed a complaint with the NCCA against Mr. Fuerstman for libel, slander, and interference with NCCA economic advantages and relationships (contact by both Mr. Fuerstman and Mr. Hales with the Blake Hotel, which has hosted the tournament three times, and now will not return NCCA phone calls or e-mails from me or Mr. Wheeless). Mr. Wheeless alleged that Mr. Hales colluded with Mr. Fuerstman. He requested sanctions against Mr. Fuerstman and Mr. Hales by the NCCA. The complaints were served on Mr. Fuerstman and Mr. Hales.

All during May, June and part of July 2009, I attempted to reach a settlement which would avoid a full scale adversarial administrative process which could take a long period of time. I chose to negotiate with Mr. Hales and  Mr. Fuerstman.   

As a result of these negotiations, Mr. Wheeless has agreed and the Board concurs to dismiss the complaints against Mr. Fuerstman and Mr. Hales with prejudice; that is, without right to refile the same complaint. Please note that Mr. Fuerstman agrees that Mr. Hales had a minimal role in this matter and was instrumental is its resolution with Mr. Wheeless and the Board.

                                Settlement with Fuerstman and Hales

Both Mr. Fuerstman and Mr. Hales, after review of the internal audit I presented them, agree with the results. Neither Mr. Fuerstman nor Mr. Hales knew before, but now recognize that the law does not allow unrestricted demands for an audit by a member. They were both also unaware of the legal issues involved in internet libel which will be addressed later in this article. They both say this will not happen again.  They both regret having been a part of the inception of this investigation which led to public internet discussions which went way beyond what was appropriate.  They have both unequivocally stated that had they known the law on these matters and that the public discussion would escalate to the level it has, they never would have been involved in the first place. They both has apologized to Mr. Wheeless and the members of the Board  for their roles in this matter and the apologies have been accepted without reservation. The Board is pleased that this controversy is cleared up. Both Mr. Fuerstman and Mr. Hales are and have been important contributors to North Carolina chess and the Board looks forward to working with them on positive matters again.

I have published two legal opinions which address the rights of members in non-profit organizations to financial records of that organization and the other on the law of libel and slander in the context of such an organization. A brief summary of those opinions are as follows.

Very simply, a member does not have a legal right to demand an audit of the financial records of the Association, statutorily or in the by-laws. See my legal opinion. Also, I have a published a legal opinion on internet libel which I encourage all to read. Very important to anyone who expresses opinions of any kind on the internet. 

I have sent a copy of this article and my legal opinions to Mr. Fuerstman to post on his website and he has agreed to post them for a period of one month. The NCCA will do the same and then both posts will be permanently removed.

                                              Financial Statement

The Board of the NCCA is not required to make financial statements to the members except once a year. The required financial statement is an annual report of income and expenses and/or a balance sheet. This is clearly stated in the By-Laws and under North Carolina law it is the only financial statement a member can demand. Members cannot demand an audit which is what Mr. Fuerstman wants. Please see my legal memorandum on this for the rational reasons which restrict the member’s rights. This exception of publishing a financial statement on this particular tournament is being made only for the purpose of clearing Randy Wheeless’ reputation.

Attached is a summary prepared by the Board and what follows is a detailed explanation of the procedures taken and the records examined.

1.  Payment to the Blake Hotel:  Mr. Wheeless entered into a contract with the Blake Hotel which required him to pay for the use of tournament rooms. This is the issue of the $4,036.51 which Mr. Fuerstman as referred to in his articles. This was in fact paid to the Blake Hotel. The contract with the Blake Hotel specifically provides for payment of the playing rooms. There are two merchant copies of two separate charges on Mr. Wheeless’ credit card. One is dated 02/18/09 in the amount of $3,000 which was a deposit on the rooms and the other is dated 03/10/09 in the amount of $1,036.51. The first charge was by faxing the credit card information to the Hotel and the other was a charge added to his Hotel account at the conclusion of the tournament - normal procedure. There is an banquet check invoice from the Blake presented to Mr. Wheeless at the conclusion of the tournament and it shows the credit of $3,000 previously charged to his card and the balance owed of $1,036.51.  President Beaman and I separately and at different times were entrusted by Mr. Wheeless with his online banking password which shows the activity on the credit card he used. He and I both came to the total and absolute conclusion that the charges were indeed made on his card, that he paid the balance off from his reimbursement from Mr. Hoekstra, the NCCA Secretary Treasurer, and that there were no “charge backs” or “credits” given back.

2. So What Was the Profit?  The profit was $8,578. There were deposits into the NCCA account of $15,111 and checks written for $6,533 as follows: Mr. Wheeless’ reimbursement $4,036; Tim Champion as Tournament Director $600 and Mr. Rogers for trophies $1,897.  But that is not the whole story. The actual amount tournament income was $17,817 so there is a difference in the NCCA checking account deposits as follows: tournament income of $17,817 less the $15,111 actually deposited for a difference of $2,706.   This is accounted for as follows: $610 was paid in cash to the floor TD’s and helpers and $500 was paid in cash to out of town coaches as incentives to participate and to stay in the hotel rather than going to a cheaper hotel. I do the same thing in my tournaments for my helpers and also to get certain individuals to come to the tournament, stay in the hotel and bring players with them - I call them my “boosters”. It is a good business practice. That leaves $1,596 to account for.  PayPal was used to pay certain expenses as follows: USCF rating fee of $387, USCF memberships of $472, Crown Awards (additional trophies) $120, Office Supplies $65 and PayPal fees of $527 and a $25 refund of an entry fee. The PayPal account zeroes out.

3. All Other Financial Issues: All issues about Thad Rogers' bill, a totally trustworthy businessman and friend to North Carolina chess, the free entries and the team rooms are all resolved with all parties.

4. How Much Money Did Mr. Wheeless Make?: Less than zero. He lost money on it with time, effort, gas, food, phone calls, postage, etc. I have run tournaments for the NCCA and never made a dime. It is volunteer work and Mr. Wheeless took this task on for the purpose of leaving a financial legacy to the NCCA.  Prior tournaments were run by local scholastic organizations and the NCCA merely collected $9 per player and the local organization kept any remaining profits. Since no scholastic group was interested in the 2009 event, Mr. Wheeless decided that the NCCA should run it. This gave the NCCA an opportunity to boost its revenues, which have never been healthier due in large part to the efforts of Mr. Wheeless as an NCCA officer and now as a NCCA volunteer. Usually the NCCA makes only about $1,000 from the event after paying out the scholastic scholarship and the expenses to the players representing North Carolina at the Denker and Polgar national scholastic tournaments. This year, after payment of these expenses, there will be an extra $4,000 in the NCCA account.

5. Was this an NCCA event? Of course it was. Mr. Wheeless did this for the NCCA because he is a tireless volunteer. However, it was Mr. Wheeless’ event in that he single handedly organized the event for the NCCA.

6. The Board’s Conclusion. Mr. Wheeless is totally cleared of any alleged wrongdoing,  his bookkeeping methods are consistent with common practice in these types of events, he exercised good judgment with regard to financial decisions and the Board is grateful for his service.

7. My Conclusion. In the legal profession, we have a saying about settlement: if both parties are dissatisfied then it is a good settlement. In settlement, no party gets exactly what they want. However, both parties, in settlement, control their destinies. If left to the judgment of others then you lose control of your destiny. Settlement mends all fences, accepts all responsibilities, forgives but does not forget the lessons learned.

Wilder Wadford
